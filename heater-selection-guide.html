Sauna Heater Selection Guide

The 50,000-Foot View. Everything a new PPW sales rep needs to understand how sauna heaters work, what brands we carry, and how to match the right heater to the right customer — before diving into individual brand guides.

Chapter 1: What Is a Sauna Heater?

Before you can sell a sauna heater, you need to understand what it actually does. This chapter starts from absolute zero.

A sauna heater is the heart of any sauna. It's the appliance that creates the heat. Without it, you just have a nicely insulated wooden box. Think of it like a furnace in a house — the room is the shell, the heater is the engine that makes it work.

Sauna heaters do two things:

  1. Heat the air inside the sauna room to between 150°F and 200°F (65°C–95°C)
  2. Heat sauna stones so you can pour water on them to create löyly (steam) — more on this in a minute

Key Definition — Löyly (LOY-loo): The Finnish word for the burst of steam that rises when you pour water over hot sauna stones. This isn't just moisture — it's the soul of the sauna experience. The quality of löyly is what separates a "meh" sauna from a transformative one. Better heaters with more stone mass produce softer, longer-lasting löyly.

Why Does the Heater Matter So Much?

Here's the reality most customers don't understand when they start shopping: the heater determines the quality of the sauna experience more than any other single component. The wood, the benches, the lighting — those are important. But the heater controls:

  • How the room heats up — fast and even, or slow with cold spots?
  • How the steam feels — harsh and biting, or soft and enveloping?
  • How stable the temperature stays — does it swing wildly or hold steady?
  • How long the heat lasts — does it disappear the moment you throw water, or does it absorb and radiate?

How to Frame This:

The core concept: The heater is to a sauna what an engine is to a car. You can have beautiful leather seats and a gorgeous paint job, but if the engine is weak, the driving experience suffers.

What the customer needs to understand: Investing in the heater is investing in the experience itself — every single sauna session for the next 10-20 years.

PPW framework to apply: Gap Selling — the customer's desired state is a transformative sauna experience, and the heater is the single biggest factor in getting them there.

Who Buys Sauna Heaters?

Your customers generally fall into three buckets:

  1. Building a new sauna from scratch — they need everything. They're often working with a contractor or doing a DIY kit. This is your biggest opportunity to guide the full setup.
  2. Upgrading an existing heater — they already have a sauna but want a better experience. These buyers already know what they want to improve. 70-80% of Harvia buyers eventually come back and upgrade to a premium brand.
  3. Replacing a broken heater — urgency purchase. They need something that fits their existing setup (same voltage, similar footprint). Speed matters here.

Chapter 2: Electric vs. Wood-Burning Heaters

Every sauna heater falls into one of two categories: electric or wood-burning. Understanding the difference is fundamental — it's the first fork in the road for every customer conversation.

Electric Heaters

Electric heaters use heating elements (metal coils, like a heavy-duty version of a toaster) to heat sauna stones and the room. You plug them into an electrical circuit, set a temperature, and they do the rest.

Key Definition — Heating Element: A resistive metal coil inside the heater that converts electricity into heat. When current flows through it, it gets extremely hot. Electric sauna heaters contain multiple elements stacked or arranged around the stones.

Why customers choose electric:

  • Convenience — turn it on, set a temperature, walk away. Some models have WiFi control so you can preheat from your phone.
  • Indoor-friendly — no chimney, no smoke, no ash. Works in basements, bathrooms, and commercial spaces.
  • Precise temperature control — digital controllers hold the room within a few degrees.
  • Lower maintenance — no wood to chop, no ash to clean, no chimney to inspect.

The tradeoff: Electric heaters require dedicated electrical circuits. Most sauna heaters need 240V power (the same voltage as your clothes dryer or oven). Small heaters under 5kW can sometimes run on 120V, but most require a licensed electrician to wire a dedicated circuit.

Wood-Burning Heaters

Wood-burning heaters use a firebox (combustion chamber) to burn wood logs. The heat from the fire passes through the stones and into the room. They require a chimney (flue pipe) to vent smoke.

Key Definition — Firebox: The enclosed chamber where wood burns inside a wood-fired heater. Think of it like the inside of a wood stove. The design of the firebox determines how efficiently the wood burns and how much heat reaches the stones.

Why customers choose wood-burning:

  • The ritual — building the fire, tending it, smelling the wood smoke. For many Finnish purists, this IS the sauna experience.
  • Off-grid capability — no electricity needed. Perfect for cabins, lakeside properties, rural locations.
  • Intense heat — wood fires can push higher temperatures and often produce a "softer" feeling heat.
  • Ambiance — the crackle and glow of a real fire adds something electric can't replicate.

The tradeoff: Requires a chimney installation, fire safety clearances, a supply of dry firewood, and regular ash cleanup. Not suitable for most indoor residential installs unless there's chimney access.

Chapter 3: Key Terms You Need to Know

Before we get into brands and sizing, you need a vocabulary foundation. Here are the terms that will come up in every heater conversation. Learn these and you'll sound like you know what you're talking about — because you will.

Key Definition — Kilowatt (kW): The unit used to measure a heater's power output. Think of it like horsepower for a car. A 6kW heater has less "oomph" than a 9kW heater — it heats a smaller space. Most residential sauna heaters range from 3kW to 18kW. Commercial heaters can go higher.

Key Definition — Cubic Feet (cu ft): How we measure sauna room size. Length × Width × Height in feet. A 6' × 6' × 7' sauna = 252 cubic feet. This number is what determines how many kW you need.

Key Definition — Stone Mass (kg): The weight of sauna stones the heater holds. More stone mass = more thermal energy stored = softer, longer-lasting steam. Think of stone mass like a thermal battery — a bigger battery stores more energy and releases it more gradually. A heater with 20kg of stones will give you a quick burst of steam. A heater with 80kg (180 lbs) will give you deep, rolling waves of löyly.

Key Definition — Heat Stratification: Hot air rises, cold air sinks. In a sauna, this means the ceiling can be 200°F while the floor is 120°F. This is normal, but extreme stratification makes the sauna uncomfortable (your head is roasting while your feet are cold). Good heater placement and certain technologies (like Saunum's air blending) reduce this problem.

Key Definition — Combi Heater: A heater that is both a traditional sauna heater AND a steam generator in one unit. It has a separate water tank that produces steam independently of throwing water on stones. This lets you run the sauna in traditional dry mode, wet (löyly) mode, or pure steam mode. Harvia's Virta Combi line is our main combi offering.

Key Definition — Controller: The device that controls the heater's temperature, timer, and (on some models) lighting and steam. Can be a simple dial built into the heater, a wall-mounted panel, or a WiFi-connected app. Some heaters include a controller; others require you to purchase one separately — always check.

Key Definition — UL Listed: The heater has been tested and certified by Underwriters Laboratories for electrical safety. This matters because many U.S. building codes and insurance companies require UL listing. Not all imported heaters have this certification. When a customer asks "is it safe?" — UL listing is your answer.

Key Definition — Ventilation: Proper airflow in and out of the sauna room. Saunas need a fresh air intake (usually low, near the heater), an exhaust vent (usually high, on the opposite wall), and a drying vent near the ceiling — keep CLOSED during use, open after the session to let moisture escape and prevent mold. Without ventilation, the sauna becomes stuffy, the air quality drops, and the heater works harder. It's not a heater spec per se — but it affects heater performance, so you'll discuss it often.

Chapter 10: How to Size a Heater

This is one of the most important skills you'll develop. An undersized heater can't heat the room. An oversized heater wastes money and can overheat the space. Getting it right builds trust with the customer — they see you as a knowledgeable consultant, not just a salesperson.

The Basic Formula

The core calculation is simple:

Room Volume (cu ft) ÷ 50 = Minimum kW needed

This is the baseline. Adjustment factors below may increase the requirement.

Example: A sauna room that is 6 ft × 6 ft × 7 ft = 252 cubic feet. 252 ÷ 50 = 5.04 kW. So you'd recommend at least a 6 kW heater (always round up to the next available size).

Adjustment Factors

The basic formula assumes a perfectly insulated, indoor sauna with no windows. Real life is rarely that clean. Here are the factors that increase your kW requirement:

Factor Adjustment Why
Uninsulated or poorly insulated walls Add 25–50% more kW Heat escapes through the walls. The heater has to work harder to maintain temperature.
Glass door or glass wall Add ~1.5 cu ft per sq ft of glass to the room volume Glass is a poor insulator and radiates heat outward. Treat each square foot of glass as if it adds 1.5 cubic feet to your room.
Concrete, brick, or stone surfaces Add ~1.8 cu ft per sq ft of exposed masonry Masonry absorbs enormous amounts of heat before the room warms up. It's like trying to fill a bigger bucket.
Outdoor placement Add 10–20% more kW Ambient cold (especially in winter) increases heat loss through the walls.
Log cabin walls (not insulated) Multiply total room volume by 1.5 Exposed log walls are thick and absorb a lot of heat energy before the room reaches sauna temperatures.
Ceiling above 7 feet Already factored into volume, but consider the extra stratification Higher ceilings mean more hot air pooling above head height. The effective "sauna zone" takes longer to heat.

Worked Example with Adjustments

Customer Scenario:

"I'm building a 7' × 8' × 7' sauna in my backyard. It has a full glass door (about 15 sq ft of glass) and it's going in a shed that isn't insulated."

Calculation:

  • Base volume: 7 × 8 × 7 = 392 cu ft
  • Glass adjustment: 15 sq ft × 1.5 = +22.5 cu ft → 414.5 cu ft
  • Outdoor/uninsulated adjustment: 414.5 × 1.4 (splitting the difference) = ~580 cu ft effective
  • kW needed: 580 ÷ 50 = 11.6 kW → recommend a 12 kW heater

Pro Tip: When in doubt, size UP, not down. An 8kW heater in a room that needs 9kW will struggle, cycle constantly, and never feel quite right. A 10.5kW heater in that same room will heat up faster, hold temperature more comfortably, and create a better experience. The customer will never complain about a heater that's slightly oversized — but they'll definitely notice one that's too small.

Watch Out: Never guess on sizing. Always ask for the room dimensions. "What's the rough size of your sauna space?" is one of the first questions in the qualifying checklist for a reason. If a customer doesn't know their dimensions, encourage them to measure before you make a recommendation. A wrong recommendation erodes trust fast.

Chapter 13: The Heater Investment Reframe

This is one of the most powerful concepts in your toolkit. Memorize the principle and adapt it to your own words.

The Problem

Customers often under-invest in the heater. They'll spend $7,000–$15,000 on a sauna room (kit, installation, tile work, lighting) and then try to save $300 on the heater — the one component that determines 80% of the experience.

The Reframe

"A $500 heater in a $7,000 sauna is like putting a 2-liter engine in a Bentley."

The room looks beautiful. The heater struggles. Every session is a reminder that they cut corners on the one thing that matters most.

How to Frame This:

The core concept: The heater is an investment in every future sauna session. Over 10 years, even a $500 price difference between a basic and a premium heater works out to pennies per session. But the experience difference is night and day.

What the customer needs to understand: They're not comparing a $600 heater to a $1,200 heater. They're comparing 10 years of "good enough" sessions to 10 years of incredible sessions. The cost-per-session math makes the premium option look like a no-brainer.

Chapter 15: Electrical Basics for Sales

You don't need to be an electrician. But you need to understand enough to have a basic conversation and avoid recommending something the customer can't install.

Key Definition — Voltage (V): Think of voltage as the "pressure" of electricity. In North America, homes have two standard voltages: 120V (standard wall outlets — what you plug a lamp into) and 240V (heavy-duty circuits — what your clothes dryer or oven runs on). Most sauna heaters need 240V.

Key Definition — Amperage (Amps): If voltage is pressure, amperage is the "volume" of electricity flowing. Higher kW heaters draw more amps. A 6kW heater draws about 25 amps. A 10.5kW heater draws about 44 amps. The customer's electrical panel needs a breaker big enough to handle the load.

Key Definition — Dedicated Circuit: An electrical circuit that serves only the sauna heater — nothing else is on it. This is required for safety and performance. An electrician runs a new wire from the electrical panel directly to the heater. This is where Peak Protection Concierge shines — PPW coordinates the electrician for free.

Watch Out: Never tell a customer they can install the heater themselves unless it's a plug-in 120V model. Hardwired 240V heaters MUST be installed by a licensed electrician. Incorrect wiring is a fire hazard and voids the warranty. If a customer asks "can I do it myself?" — the answer for 240V is always "the wiring needs a licensed electrician, but we'll coordinate one for you at no extra charge through our Peak Protection Concierge."

The Key Electrical Question

The single most important thing to find out: Does the customer have (or can they add) a 240V circuit with enough amperage? If yes, the world is open. If they're limited to 120V, you're looking at small heaters only (under 5kW), which limits room size to about 200 cubic feet.
