Chapter 2: Understanding Wood-Burning Sauna Stoves (Foundations)

If you've never worked with wood-burning sauna stoves before, this chapter will give you everything you need. We'll define every term before we use it.

What Is a Wood-Burning Sauna Stove?

A wood-burning sauna stove (also called a wood-fired stove) is a heating device that burns firewood to heat a sauna room. It's the oldest and most traditional way to heat a sauna. Instead of plugging into an electrical outlet, you load wood into a firebox (the combustion chamber), light it, and the fire heats the stove body and the stones sitting on top.

Key Definition — Firebox: The enclosed chamber inside the stove where wood burns. Think of it like the fire pit inside a wood stove or fireplace. Narvi stoves have glass doors on the firebox so you can watch the flames.

How Does It Work?

Here's the step-by-step process, explained simply:

  1. Load firewood into the firebox through the stove door. Narvi stoves use firewood no longer than 13 inches (33 cm) and about 2 inches (5 cm) in diameter.
  2. Light the fire. As wood burns, it produces heat measured in kilowatts (kW).
  3. Heat rises through the stove body into the stones stacked on top and around the stove.
  4. Stones absorb and store heat — they act like a thermal battery. The more stone mass, the more heat energy is stored.
  5. Pour water on the hot stones to create löyly (the Finnish word for sauna steam). The water instantly flashes into steam, filling the room with moist heat.

Key Definition — Kilowatt (kW): A measurement of heat output. Think of it like horsepower for a car engine — the higher the kW, the more heat the stove produces and the larger the sauna it can heat. A 13kW stove heats a smaller room; a 50kW stove can heat a large commercial space.

Key Definition — Löyly: (pronounced "LOY-loo") The steam that rises when you throw water on hot sauna stones. In Finnish culture, good löyly is soft, enveloping, and never harsh. Stone mass and stone type directly affect löyly quality.

Key Components of a Wood-Burning Setup

The Stove

The stove itself — the steel body with the firebox, stone compartment, and glass door. All Narvi stoves are floor-mounted, meaning they sit on the sauna floor (not wall-hung).

The Stones

Sauna stones sit on top of and around the stove. They absorb heat from the fire and release it as radiant warmth and steam. Narvi uses olivine diabase stones.

Key Definition — Olivine Diabase: A type of volcanic rock known for being extremely heat-resistant and durable. It doesn't crack as easily as regular river rocks when exposed to repeated heating and water dousing. Think of it as the difference between tempered glass and regular glass — same concept, but engineered to handle thermal stress.

Narvi offers two stone types:

  • Split-Face Stones — rough-cut surface, more traditional look, excellent steam production
  • Round Stones — smooth and rounded, more modern aesthetic, also excellent steam

The Flue (Chimney Connection)

Since wood-burning stoves produce smoke, they need a flue — a pipe that channels smoke out of the building. All Narvi stoves offer top or back flue connection, giving installation flexibility.

Key Definition — Flue: The pipe or duct that carries smoke and combustion gases from the stove out through the roof or wall. Think of it like the exhaust pipe on a car. Every wood-burning stove requires one — this is a critical installation consideration.

Safety and Certification

All Narvi stoves carry a CE safety listing.

Key Definition — CE Marking: A European conformity marking indicating the product meets EU safety, health, and environmental requirements. It's the European equivalent of a UL listing in North America. CE-marked products have been tested and certified to meet strict safety standards.

Watch Out: CE is a European certification, not a North American UL listing. Some local building codes in the U.S. and Canada may require UL-listed products. Always advise customers to check with their local building department and installer before purchasing. Do NOT claim these are UL listed — they are not.

Pro Tip: Lead with CE certification proactively. Customers who do online research will find this and ask. Getting ahead of it builds trust: "I want to be upfront — Narvi stoves carry CE certification, which is the European safety standard. It's not a UL listing, which is the North American standard. Most sauna installations don't require UL, but I always recommend checking with your local building department to be sure."

Why Stone Mass Matters: Heat Stratification

Here's something most customers don't know: in a typical sauna, hot air rises and stays at the top. Your head cooks while your feet stay cool. This is called heat stratification, and it's the reason people lay down on sauna benches — not because they're tired, but because they want their entire body at the same temperature.

Wood-burning stoves with high stone mass (like the Narvi Inari Plus with 198 lbs of stone) address this differently than low-stone heaters. All those stones act as a massive radiant heat source, radiating warmth in all directions rather than just blasting hot air upward. The result: more even heat distribution, longer-lasting warmth, and gentler steam. When a customer says "I want really good steam," what they're actually describing is the experience that high stone mass delivers.

How to Frame: Wood-Burning vs. Electric Conversation

Mental model: This is not a "which is better" conversation — it's a "which experience do you want" conversation. Electric is convenience; wood-burning is ritual. Neither is wrong. Your job is to help the customer discover which one matches their vision.

Approach: Use the campfire-vs-microwave analogy to make the distinction intuitive. Never knock electric — PPW sells both. Instead, explore what the customer's ideal sauna session looks like. If they describe fire, aroma, tradition, or off-grid use, guide them toward wood-burning. If they describe push-button convenience, respect that and offer electric options.

PPW framework: The Gap — explore what "sauna experience" means to them. Their answer reveals whether they're a wood-burning customer or an electric customer. Let their words guide the recommendation, not yours.

Why This Matters for Sales: Understanding wood-burning fundamentals makes you credible. When you can explain kW, flue routing, and stone mass in plain language, the customer trusts that you know what you're talking about. That trust is the foundation of every recommendation you'll make in the next chapters.

The LAER Framework Refresher

Key Definition — LAER:

  • Listen — Let the customer express their full concern. Don't interrupt.
  • Acknowledge — Show you understand. "That's a fair concern..."
  • Explore — Ask questions to understand the real concern behind the surface objection.
  • Respond — Address the real concern with relevant information.

The key insight: the first thing a customer says is rarely the real objection. LAER helps you dig to the root.

Top Customer Questions

"Is this safe to use? It's a fire inside my sauna..."

Ventilation: The 3-Vent System for Wood-Burning Saunas

Proper ventilation is critical for wood-burning sauna stoves. Unlike electric heaters, wood-burning stoves consume oxygen for combustion and produce combustion gases. Without adequate airflow, the stove will not burn efficiently, the sauna air quality will suffer, and moisture will damage the room over time. Every wood-burning Narvi installation should include these three vents:

VentLocationPurposeDuring UseAfter Use
1. Fresh Air Intake Low on the wall, near the stove Supplies combustion air to the stove and fresh oxygen to the room OPEN OPEN
2. Exhaust Vent Opposite wall from the stove, under the bench Removes spent air and draws fresh air across the room. Must be LARGER than the intake vent OPEN OPEN
3. Drying Vent Near the ceiling, opposite wall from the stove Removes humid air after the sauna session to prevent mold and wood rot CLOSED OPEN

Watch Out: The drying vent must be CLOSED during use — if left open, it will pull all the heat out through the ceiling. It's only opened after the session to dry the room. The exhaust vent must be larger than the intake to create proper airflow. These are common installation mistakes — flag them proactively with the customer and their installer.

Pro Tip: Wood-burning stoves need combustion air — this is the single most important reason the fresh air intake vent exists. Without it, the stove starves for oxygen, burns inefficiently, produces more smoke, and can create negative pressure in the room. If a customer reports that their wood-burning stove "doesn't burn well" or "smokes too much," the first question to ask is: "Do you have a fresh air intake vent near the stove?"
